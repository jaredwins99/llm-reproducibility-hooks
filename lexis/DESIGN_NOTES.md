# Lexis — Design Notes

Hard-won principles, ≤18 words per bullet.

## What a lexis is

- A lexis is a bounded repertoire: defined by what speakers CANNOT say, not by jargon.
- Simplified speech is a primary direction — missing vocabulary, low abstraction, absent hedges.
- Occupational jargon variants (hunter vs rancher) are shallow; constraint level is the deep axis.
- Losing the vocabulary of nuance may remove the ability to hedge — that's the hypothesis.

## Who knows what (information hygiene)

- A (demand) knows nothing of roles or registers.
- B (role) never sees the topic; steered only by rotating diversity hints.
- C (lexis) never sees the topic; all examples topic-neutral.
- D (translator) sees demand + lexis spec; must not add stance content.
- E (respondent) is NEVER told the register's rules — knowing the bounds would break inhabitation.
- D's examples are the only teacher: enough priming that E follows suit unprompted.

## Translation

- D stays strictly inside the repertoire; no smuggling precise words to preserve meaning.
- Lossiness is the phenomenon, not a bug — inexpressible concepts get concretized or dropped.
- The answer-format line survives translation verbatim; it is the parse contract.

## Arms & measurement

- Three arms: lexis, neutral_translation, control — separates rewording effects from register effects.
- A–D artifacts shared within trial; only E repeats per arm (paired design).
- Unparseable answers are data (hedging escape), never errors to retry.
- Topics human-vetted; bank spans corpus-rehearsal spectrum (anchored ↔ thin).
- Prediction: answer shift grows as corpus rehearsal shrinks and register constraint tightens.

## Roles

- Role bank spans constraint spectrum: heavily restricted ↔ elaborated, not occupational variety.
- Hedging availability × abstraction ceiling is the key design axis against forced answers.
